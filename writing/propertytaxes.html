Leveraging property tax policy to combat the housing shortage
October 11, 2023
Property tax policy can move us toward solving the housing shortage in America across all segments of affordability.

To do this, property tax policy should:
  • Value residential real estate utilizing the cost approach to valuation.
    The cost approach to valuation fairly values residential real estate for property tax purposes by reducing market volatility in the tax base, providing a predictable valuation model for taxpayers, and requiring tax rate setting authorities to budget prudently without the benefit that economic expansions confer on the tax base. Notably, all property types should be valued for property tax purposes using the cost approach. Nevada has demonstrated that this is possible and leads to equitable outcomes.

  • Assess all property at 100% of value.
    Preferential assessment that favors residential housing, e.g., Arizona's 10% assessment ratio for residential property vs. 16.5% assessment ratio for commercial property, does not incentivize local zoning for residential uses, especially multifamily housing. All property should be valued at 100% of value so that local governments are not incentivized to consider property tax revenue when making local zoning and planning decisions. This point also applies to tax rate preferences for residential property (e.g., city and town tax rates in Massachusetts that favor primary single-family houses).

  • Provide property tax abatements for affordable rental housing.
    Property tax abatements are powerful tools to incentivize private investment. State and local governments should abate property taxes for affordable rental housing. A new program in Florida provides a good example. The Missing Middle property tax exemption program provides a 100% property tax exemption for units serving up to 80% AMI income levels and a 75% property tax exemption for units serving greater than 80% AMI to 120% AMI income levels. The exemption attaches to units vs. the overall project providing developers and operators with flexibility and requires a modest commitment of at least 70 units and three years of affordability. States should look to Florida's Missing Middle program in crafting property tax abatement programs to incentivize new affordable rental housing.
